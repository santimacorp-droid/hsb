ABSTRACT
Help-seeking behavior plays a vital role in students’ academic success and
well-being as it influences their utilization of institutional support systems in higher
education. This study employed a quantitative design using descriptive, comparative, and
correlational methods to examine the level of help-seeking behavior, the extent of utilization
of available support systems, their differences across profile variables, and their relationship
among 361 Medical Technology students of the University of La Salette, Inc. Findings
revealed that students exhibited a moderate level of help-seeking behavior and moderate to
high utilization of support systems, with Prefect of Discipline and Office of Student Services
and Affairs being the most frequently accessed services, while Faculty Support, Health and
Development Center, and Department of Student Services were only moderately utilized.
No significant differences were found in help-seeking behavior and support system
utilization when grouped according to sex and year level, while significant differences
emerged across departments. Furthermore, a significant positive relationship was found
between help-seeking behavior and the utilization of all support systems, indicating that
students who are more inclined to seek help are more likely to engage with institutional
services. The study concludes that help-seeking behavior is generally moderate and
influenced more by departmental context than by sex or year level, and that stronger
help-seeking tendencies are associated with greater utilization of support systems. It is
recommended that the university strengthen proactive help-seeking initiatives, enhance
awareness and accessibility of underutilized services, implement department-based
interventions, and improve the integration and coordination of student support systems to
promote holistic student development and well-being.

Keywords: academic help-seeking, student engagement, institutional support utilization,

INTRODUCTION
Mental health concerns among college students continue to intensify in
academically demanding programs. Students are often exposed to heavy academic
workloads, coursework requirements, and performance-based requirements that require
sustained focus and high performance, increasing their vulnerability to stress and
emotional strain. Tan et al (2025) showed that these circumstances of education led to
self-stigma which contributed to the students' discouragement in seeking mental health
support. These circumstances provide context for understanding how students perceive
and manage their academic experiences, as well as how they access and engage with
available support systems.
This study focuses on students’ tendency to seek assistance as a key factor in
understanding how they cope with academic and psychological demands. Willingness to
ask for help reflects how students perceive their own difficulties and their openness to
engaging with others for guidance or care. Psychological barriers such as self-stigma,
fear of judgment, and limited confidence in formal services may influence this behavior,
especially in high-pressure learning settings. Therefore, the research investigates the
level of help-seeking behavior among university students and how it differs when
categorized by specific traits.
In parallel, the study examines the spectrum of support systems available to
students. This includes sources like

the Faculty, Health and Development Center,

Department of Student Services, Prefect of Discipline, Office of the Student Services and
Affairs. The decision to use these sources is influenced by their

availability,

approachability, and the students’ perception of their effectiveness. By identifying the
types of support systems present at the University of La Salette, Inc., and assessing the

2
extent to which these are utilized, the study seeks to describe patterns of student
engagement with these University’s services.
Given these considerations, the research explores the connection between
students’ willingness to seek help and their actual use of available support systems.The
study will also look for any differences in these factors across various student profile
groups, focusing on the relationship between help-seeking behavior and support system
utilization. Through this approach, it allows the study to examine how individual
tendencies and institutional resources interact within the academic context of university
students.

Background of the Study
Mental health concerns are prevalent among university students, with many
experiencing stress, anxiety, emotional distress, and other psychological difficulties
linked to academic demands and developmental transitions. Of particular concern are
self-harm behaviors and suicide-related risks, recognized as serious public health issues
among young adults (Tickell, 2022). Despite these challenges, many students
demonstrate limited awareness of mental health resources and low engagement in
professional help-seeking, often relying on self-care or peer support. This is supported
by Zhao et al. (2025), whose systematic review and meta-analysis of 62 studies found
that although many college students intend to seek professional help, only about
one-third actually utilize mental health services. The review identified self-reliance, lack
of time, limited knowledge of available services, perceived lack of need, concerns about
privacy, and stigma as the most common barriers preventing students from seeking
professional support. While peers can provide emotional comfort, they may lack the

3
expertise to address severe distress. Help-seeking decisions are influenced by stigma,
normalization of distress, internalized negative attitudes toward mental health services,
and perceived barriers to care, leading many students to avoid professional assistance
even when needed.

Research on help-seeking behavior and support systems has largely focused on
general student populations, emphasizing psychological barriers, attitudes toward
services, and stigma. While informative, these studies rarely consider the specific
culture, expectations, and training contexts of students across different university
programs. Similarly, studies on support systems often examine available resources
without assessing how students actually engage with them under academic or emotional
pressure. The limited integration of these constructs leaves gaps in understanding how
recognition of distress translates into the utilization of support mechanisms in specialized
programs.

Theoretical Framework
This quantitative study will be anchored on Cobb’s Social Support Theory (1976),
which states that individuals are more likely to cope effectively with stress when they
perceive themselves as cared for, valued, and supported within a social system. In the
Philippine context, university students often experience high academic demands,
including intensive coursework, laboratory requirements, and performance-based
assessments, which may increase their need for support and influence their
help-seeking behavior.

LITERATURE REVIEW
Support Systems in Higher Education
Support systems in higher education have been recognized as essential
resources for promoting students’ academic performance, psychological functioning, and
overall well-being. Social support, in particular, serves as a critical component of these
systems by providing emotional, informational, and practical resources that help students
navigate the challenges of university life. A systematic review examining 51 empirical
studies published between 2010 and 2024 analyzed the influence of social support on
undergraduate students’ mental health and well-being. The review found that social
support exerts both direct and indirect effects on student outcomes. Direct effects
include improved psychological and emotional well-being, reduced stress, and healthier
behaviors, while indirect effects involve enhancing resilience, self-esteem, and life
satisfaction through emotional and informational mediation (Ruihua et al., 2024).
Chen, Zhu, and Xiao (2023) examined the interrelationships between social
support, sense of security, family cultural capital, and academic self-efficacy among
1,119 college students. Using a multivariate moderated mediation model, the study
found that social support significantly increased students’ sense of security, which in turn
enhanced academic self-efficacy. In addition, social support was shown to have a direct
positive effect on academic self-efficacy, while a sense of security partially mediated the
relationship between social support and academic self-efficacy. These findings provide
empirical evidence on the mechanisms through which social support contributes to
students’ academic confidence and engagement.
Furthermore, Vicarya et al.(2024) conducted a systematic review examining the
role and impact of social support for university students with self-reported or diagnosed

10
mental health problems. The study included ten studies with a total of 3,669 participants
and found that high levels of social support were significantly associated with reduced
suicide risk, depression, anxiety, and psychological distress. The study also identified
barriers to accessing support, highlighting how variations in availability and engagement
with support resources influence mental health outcomes among students.
Valenti and Branham (2024) explored the relationship between social support
and perceived stress among 203 undergraduate freshman students, using both
quantitative

and

qualitative

methods.

Quantitative

assessment

employed

the

Multidimensional Scale of Perceived Social Support (MSPSS) and the Perceived Stress
Scale

(PSS-10),

while

qualitative

data

were

gathered

through

open-ended

questionnaires. The study found that students reported higher-than-average stress
levels compared to peers in the general population and that higher levels of social
support were positively correlated with increased self-efficacy and negatively correlated
with perceived helplessness.
Johnson et al. (2022) investigated how university students utilize campus support
services and whether service utilization influences academic performance and student
retention. Using a quantitative correlational design, the researchers analyzed institutional
records from foundation and undergraduate students at Qatar University, including
service usage logs and academic performance data. The study found that students who
actively engaged with support services such as academic advising, counseling, tutoring,
and career guidance generally achieved higher grades and were more likely to remain
enrolled in the university. These findings imply that support systems are essential
components of student success, emphasizing the need for higher education institutions
to improve awareness and accessibility of available services.

11
Martín-Arbós et al. (2021) conducted a systematic review to examine academic
help-seeking behavior among adolescents and university students and its relationship
with institutional support systems. The study reviewed 25 peer-reviewed journal articles
and focused on variables such as perceived social support, accessibility of services, and
barriers to help-seeking. Findings revealed that students were more likely to seek
assistance when support services were visible, accessible, and trusted. The review
highlighted that simply offering support services is insufficient if students are unaware of
them or hesitant to use them. Therefore, universities should actively promote support
systems and create student-friendly environments that encourage service utilization.
Samuel and Kamenetsky (2022) explored help-seeking preferences among
first-year undergraduate students and the factors influencing their attitudes toward
mental health services. Using a quantitative descriptive-correlational design, data were
collected from 167 first-year university students in Canada through online surveys.
Results indicated that students preferred seeking support from family and friends rather
than formal university services. Cost, limited availability, and uncertainty about service
effectiveness were identified as major barriers. The study implies that universities should
strengthen both formal support services and informal support networks to improve
students’ adjustment and well-being during their transition to higher education.
Li et al. (2023) conducted a systematic literature review to synthesize existing
research on college students’ academic help-seeking behavior and the role of academic
support systems. The review analyzed 55 studies published between 2012 and 2022
and identified themes related to academic performance, support resources, self-efficacy,
and learning strategies. Findings showed that support systems such as faculty members,
peers, family, and academic centers significantly enhance students’ willingness to seek
help and improve their academic engagement and achievement. These results suggest

12
that comprehensive support systems are crucial for fostering student confidence,
learning effectiveness, and academic success.
Ryahin et al. (2026) examined the relationship between perceived social support,
self-esteem, and help-seeking behavior among nursing students. Using a quantitative
correlational design and standardized questionnaires, the researchers assessed
students’ perceptions of support from family, friends, and educators. The study found
that students with stronger social support networks were more likely to seek help and
exhibited higher levels of self-esteem. This finding implies that emotional and
interpersonal support systems play an important role in encouraging students to seek
assistance and cope effectively with academic and personal challenges.
Qualitative analysis further revealed that students primarily sought support from
family, friends, and counseling services. Barriers to seeking support included
self-reliance, fear of burdening others, and trust issues. These findings highlight the role
of social support in shaping students’ psychological functioning and the importance of
recognizing and addressing obstacles to support access. The study underscores the
multifaceted ways in which institutional, peer, and familial support systems contribute to
student well-being in higher education contexts
Fronda et al. (2025) investigated the predictive relationship between perceived
social support and mental health among 104 first-year college students at a state
university in Pampanga, Philippines. Using a quantitative predictive correlational design,
the study found a weak but significant negative relationship (p < 0.001, R = -0.48)
between perceived social support and mental health, indicating that perceived support
accounted for 23% of the variance in students’ mental health outcomes. Data were
analyzed using JAMOVI version 2.4.14, and respondents were selected through

13
purposive sampling. The study highlights the nuanced role of social support, showing
that while support can benefit students, excessive or misaligned support may contribute
to distress and hinder students’ autonomy and stress-coping abilities.

Segbenya and Bervell (2024) investigated the factors that influence students'
decisions to seek academic help and utilize university support services. The study
found that students' recognition of their academic needs, encouragement from their
social networks, and awareness of available campus resources were the strongest
predictors of help-seeking behavior. Although emotional distress increased students'
need for assistance, it did not necessarily lead them to seek help unless they clearly
knew where to obtain support and felt comfortable accessing it. The findings
highlight the importance of higher education institutions in promoting support
services, increasing their visibility, and creating an environment where students feel
safe and encouraged to seek assistance.
Pantaleon, Uy, and Dino (2022) examined the help-seeking experiences of
Filipino university students and the influence of formal and informal support systems
on their decisions to seek assistance. The study found that students usually turned
to family and friends before approaching counselors or other professional services.
The quality, accessibility, and trustworthiness of available support systems played a
significant role in encouraging students to seek help. However, barriers such as
stigma, fear of being a burden, and lack of trust in institutional services limited the
use of formal support. The findings suggest that higher education institutions should
strengthen both formal support services and peer support networks to foster a more
supportive and help-seeking campus environment.

14
The findings also emphasize the influence of support sources and environmental
factors on the effectiveness of social support. Perceived support from demanding family
environments was associated with decreased mental health outcomes among first-year
students, suggesting that the characteristics of support providers and the types of
stressors students face may shape the impact of support. This study provides evidence
on the complex interaction between social support and mental health in higher education
contexts, particularly among early college students navigating transitional challenges.
These studies emphasize the multifaceted role of social support within the
broader framework of higher education support systems. Engagement with social
networks, which includes peers, family, and institutional contacts, provides critical
resources that can buffer students against academic and social stressors. The findings
highlight the significance of structured and accessible support mechanisms in fostering
positive student experiences and enhancing the functional capacity of students to
manage the demands of university life. These insights underscore the centrality of social
support as an integral component of institutional support systems in higher education.

They have identified that family cultural capital moderates the effects of social support
on academic self-efficacy, particularly influencing both direct and indirect pathways. This
suggests that the impact of social support may vary depending on students’ familial and
cultural backgrounds. By employing validated instruments, including the Social Support
Scale, the Sense of Security Scale, and the Academic Self-efficacy Scale, the study
highlights how psychological and contextual factors interact with social support to shape
academic self-efficacy within higher education settings
Furthermore, much of the studies emphasized that social support can have both
positive and negative effects depending on its nature, quality, and accessibility. Students

15
with insufficient access to high social support were particularly vulnerable to adverse
psychological outcomes, indicating that the effectiveness of support interventions is
contingent upon meeting the distinct needs of this population. The study employed
rigorous quality assessment using the Mixed Methods Appraisal Tool and synthesized
evidence from multiple electronic and grey literature databases, demonstrating the
empirical significance of social support as a critical component of higher education
support systems .
Support systems in higher education play a vital role in promoting students’
academic success, mental health, and overall well-being. The reviewed studies
consistently showed that social support from family, peers, educators, and institutional
services enhances students’ self-efficacy, resilience, emotional well-being, and academic
engagement while reducing stress, anxiety, and psychological distress. Students who
actively utilize counseling services, academic advising, tutoring programs, and
supportive social networks tend to perform better academically and are more likely to
remain enrolled in their institutions. Furthermore, accessible, visible, and trusted support
systems encourage students to seek assistance when facing challenges, strengthening
their ability to cope with the demands of university life.
These findings imply that higher education institutions should prioritize the
development of comprehensive, student-centered support systems that address both
academic and psychological needs. Universities must ensure that support services are
well-publicized, easily accessible, and responsive to students’ diverse backgrounds and
circumstances. Strengthening connections among students, families, faculty members,
and campus services can foster a supportive learning environment that enhances
student retention, well-being, and academic achievement.

Help seeking behavior in high demand academic environments
Understanding help-seeking behavior among university students is critical for
developing effective mental health interventions in academically intensive environments.
Barkham (2021) investigated the mental health profiles of students across multiple UK
universities and identified key barriers, intentions, and preferences that shape students’
decisions to seek psychological support. The study revealed that distinct mental health
patterns exist across academic faculties and that students often experience obstacles
such as stigma, uncertainty about service effectiveness, and limited awareness of
available resources. These findings highlight the importance of examining not only the
presence of mental health concerns, but also the psychological and structural factors
that influence students’ willingness to engage with support services.
Psychological barriers, particularly self-stigma, play a critical role in shaping
college students’ mental health outcomes and their willingness to seek professional
support. In a predictive correlational study conducted among Filipino college students,
Tan et al. (2025) examined the relationship between self-stigma of seeking help and
mental well-being. Their findings revealed a significant negative association, indicating
that higher levels of self-stigma are linked to lower mental well-being and reduced
likelihood of engaging in psychological support services. This highlights how internalized
negative beliefs about mental health care can undermine students’ capacity to cope with
academic and emotional stressors, even when formal support services are available.
Help-seeking behavior is increasingly recognized as a crucial mediator in the
relationship between personal resources and student mental health. In a study
examining postgraduates, Min et al. (2022) explored the influence of self-compassion on
mental health, highlighting the mediating role of non-professional help-seeking behavior.

17
Their findings revealed that self-compassion positively predicted engagement in
help-seeking behaviors, which in turn enhanced positive mental health factors, while also
reducing psychological symptoms. This demonstrates that students who are more
self-compassionate are not only more likely to reach out for support, but also experience
better overall well-being as a result.
Canda et al. (2025) investigate how self-stigma acts as a psychological barrier to
seeking mental health support and its predictive impact on students’ well-being. By
highlighting the internal attitudes and perceptions that influence whether students reach
out for help, this study aligns closely with the objective of understanding help-seeking
behavior among students in academically demanding programs, such as Medical
Technology students at the University of La Salette, Inc. The research emphasizes that
even when support services are available, personal barriers like stigma can prevent
students from utilizing these resources effectively, increasing vulnerability to mental
health challenges.
The findings of Canda et al. (2025) are particularly relevant for the current study
because they underscore the interaction between individual psychological factors and
the use of institutional and social support systems. Their results showed a negative
correlation between self-stigma and mental well-being, highlighting that students who
perceive higher stigma are less likely to seek help and more prone to stress, anxiety, and
other mental health concerns. This provides a conceptual basis for exploring how
Medical Technology students navigate help-seeking within the context of rigorous
academic demands and clinical training, as well as how universities can design
interventions such as stigma-reduction programs and supportive counseling initiatives to
encourage proactive engagement with available support systems, thereby promoting
both mental health and academic success.

experience help-seeking, including the psychological processes of problem recognition,
decision-making, and reliance on support systems. The study highlights both informal
sources

high-pressure educational settings. Their study illustrates that the decision to seek help
is shaped by both the student’s outlook and the perceived quality of support systems,

METHODOLOGY
Research Design
The study utilized a quantitative descriptive-correlational research design to
examine students’ help-seeking behavior and their engagement with support systems.
The quantitative methods were used to collect and analyze numerical data, enabling the
identification of patterns, trends and relationships between variables.
The descriptive part presented the prevalence and patterns of help-seeking
behavior, including students’ use of support services such as from Peers, Faculty and
support services such as Human Development Center (HDC), Department of Student
Services and Affairs (DSS), Prefect of Discipline (POD) and Office of Student Services
and Affairs (OSSA). Descriptive statistics including frequencies, percentages, means
and standard deviations were used to summarize the data.
The correlational part examined the relationship between help-seeking behavior
and support system utilization. Pearson’s correlation coefficient was used for continuous
and normally distributed variables, while Spearman’s rho was applied to ordinal or
non-normally distributed variables. The findings provided insights into how support
systems influenced students’ help-seeking practices, informing strategies to improve the
accessibility, effectiveness, and utilization of support services in the University of La
Salette, Inc.

Table 3 reveals the level of help-seeking behavior among the respondents,
yielding a mean rating of 3.20 (SD = 0.76), which is interpreted as “Sometimes.” This
indicates that, in general, students demonstrate a moderate tendency to seek help when
facing academic, personal, or emotional concerns, suggesting that help-seeking is
present but not consistently practiced across situations.
Among the indicators, the highest mean rating is observed in the item “I ask for
guidance when I do not understand academic tasks or lessons” with a mean of 3.47-2.47
(SD = 1.01), interpreted as “Often.” This implies that students are more inclined to seek
assistance specifically in academic comprehension-related concerns, highlighting a
stronger reliance on support when tasks directly affect their learning performance. This
finding is consistent with Li et al. (2023), who explained that students are more likely to
seek help for academic concerns because they view help-seeking as a positive learning

48
strategy that enhances academic performance and enables them to overcome learning
difficulties. Academic concerns also carry immediate consequences, such as grades and
course requirements, motivating students to seek assistance promptly.
In contrast, the lowest mean rating is reflected in the item “I ask for help instead
of handling my concerns on my own” with a mean of 2.92 (SD = 1.08), still interpreted as
“Sometimes.” This suggests that students tend to manage their concerns independently
before seeking external support, indicating a degree of self-reliance that may limit timely
help-seeking behavior.
Collectively, the results imply that while students are somewhat receptive to
seeking assistance, their help-seeking behavior tends to occur only under specific
circumstances rather than as a regular or preventive practice. The moderate level of
help-seeking may indicate the presence of barriers such as lack of awareness of
available services, concerns about stigma, fear of judgment, limited trust in support
providers, or uncertainty regarding when and where to seek help. Consequently, some
students may choose to endure challenges independently until these concerns begin to
significantly affect their academic performance, emotional well-being, or social
functioning.
The findings further suggest the need for the University of La Salette, Inc. to
strengthen initiatives that promote a positive help-seeking culture among students. This
may include enhancing the visibility and accessibility of support services, conducting
regular orientations and awareness campaigns, fostering supportive relationships
between students and university personnel, and creating safe and nonjudgmental
environments where students feel comfortable expressing their concerns. Encouraging
early help-seeking behaviors can contribute to improved academic achievement, better

49
mental health outcomes, greater resilience, and a stronger sense of belonging within the
university community. Ultimately, cultivating proactive help-seeking practices may enable
students to address challenges more effectively and maximize the benefits of the
support systems available to them.
The systematic review conducted by Zhao et al. (2025) found that although many
university students recognize the need for support, far fewer actually utilize formal
services. The review identified self-reliance, limited awareness of available resources,
and uncertainty about professional intervention as common barriers to help-seeking. A
related observation was reported by Tan et al. (2025), who demonstrated that higher
levels of self-stigma were associated with lower mental well-being and a reduced
willingness to seek psychological support among Filipino college students. These
findings are reflected in the present study, where respondents displayed only a moderate
tendency to seek help and were more likely to ask for assistance when academic
concerns directly affected their learning. The results suggest that personal attitudes and
psychological barriers continue to influence students' decisions to seek support despite
the availability of university services.

SUMMARY, CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Summary of Findings
The study found that the respondents were predominantly female, mostly junior
students, and represented diverse academic departments, with the largest proportion
coming from, CONPHM, CEA, and CMAMP. This balanced and heterogenous
distribution provided a representative basis for examining help-seeking behavior and the
utilization of university support systems. Overall, students demonstrated a moderate
level of help seeking behavior, showing a greater tendency to seek assistance for
academic concerns while remaining relatively self-reliant in addressing other concerns.
The extent of support system utilization ranged from moderate to high, with the prefect of
discipline(POD), and the Office of Student Services and Affairs(OSSA) being the most
frequently utilized services.In contrast, Faculty Support, the Health and Development
Center (HDC), and the Department of Student Services (DSS) were only moderately
utilized, suggesting a gap between students' awareness of these services and their

76
actual use, particularly for academic and wellness-related support. Furthermore, no
significant differences in help-seeking behavior and support system utilization were
observed when respondents were grouped according to sex and year level. However,
significant differences were found across departments, indicating that departmental
culture and academic environment may influence students' help-seeking practices and
engagement with institutional support services. Finally, the findings revealed a significant
positive relationship between help-seeking behavior and the utilization of all support
systems, suggesting that students who are more willing to seek help are also more likely
to access available university support services.

Conclusions
Based on the findings, the following conclusions were derived:
1. The respondents are diverse in terms of sex, year level, and department, with a
relatively balanced distribution across academic programs. This heterogeneity
provides a strong and representative basis for analyzing help-seeking behavior
and support system utilization.
2. Students exhibit a moderate level of help-seeking behavior. They are more likely to
seek help for academic concerns but tend to be self-reliant in handling personal
and other issues, indicating that help-seeking is situational rather than habitual or
proactive.
3. Students moderately to highly utilize available support systems. POD and OSSA
are frequently utilized, while Faculty Support, HDC, and DSS are only moderately
used. This indicates that students tend to rely more on disciplinary and
administrative services than on academic and wellness-oriented support services,
and there is a gap between awareness and actual utilization, especially for HDC
and DSS.
4. There are no significant differences in help-seeking behavior and support system
utilization when grouped according to sex and year level. However, significant
differences exist across departments, indicating that academic environment and
departmental culture influence students’ help-seeking behavior and engagement
with support systems.

5. There is a significant positive relationship between help-seeking behavior
and the utilization of all available support systems. Students who are more

78
inclined to seek help are also more likely to engage with institutional support
services, particularly academic and wellness-related support.

Recommendations
Base on the findings and conclusions, the following are highly recommended:
1. The university may continue ensuring balanced representation of students in
institutional studies, consultations, and programs to maintain inclusivity and
strengthen the accuracy of student development initiatives.
2. The university should strengthen programs that promote proactive help-seeking
behavior among students. This includes mental health awareness campaigns,
orientation programs, and classroom-based initiatives that reduce stigma and
encourage students to seek help early and consistently.
3. The university should further develop the visibility, accessibility, and effectiveness
of Faculty Support, HDC, and DSS through strengthened information campaigns,
mentoring programs, counseling outreach, and student engagement initiatives.
Efforts should also focus on bridging the gap between awareness and actual
utilization of support services, while maintaining the strong engagement observed
in OSSA and POD.
4. Department-based interventions should be implemented to address variations in
help-seeking behavior and support utilization. Each college may develop tailored
strategies to strengthen student engagement, improve awareness, and enhance
utilization of support systems based on their specific needs and culture.

79
5. The university should strengthen the integration of support systems by improving
coordination among OSSA, DSS, HDC, Faculty Support, and POD. A unified
referral and support mechanism should be established to encourage continuous
and seamless utilization of services among students who exhibit help-seeking
behavior.
6. Recommendations for Key Stakeholders
Future Researchers. They are encouraged to expand the study by including
variables such as academic performance, psychological well-being, coping
strategies, and institutional climate. A mixed-methods or qualitative approach
may also be employed to explore deeper insights into students’ lived
experiences, barriers to help-seeking, and motivations. Comparative studies
across universities or regions are also recommended to enhance generalizability
and broader understanding of help-seeking behavior.

RESEARCH QUESTIONS

Research Objectives
This study aims to assess the interrelationship between the help-seeking behavior of the
students and the support systems within the University of La Salette, Inc.
​ Specifically, this study aims to answer the following questions:
1.​ What is the demographic profile of the students in terms of:
1.1 Age
1.2 Sex
1.3 Year level
1.4 Department
2.​ What types and sources of support systems are utilized by the students in terms of:
2.1 Faculty
2.2 Health and Development Center (HDC)
2.3 Department of Student Services (DSS)
2.4 Prefect of Discipline (POD)
2.5 Office of the Student Services and Affairs (OSSA)
3.​ What is the level of Help Seeking behavior among the respondents in terms of their
help-seeking practices and the frequency with which they seek help?
4.​ What is the extent of utilization of available support systems among students of the
University of La Salette, Inc.?
5.​ Is there a significant difference among the respondents based on their help-seeking
behavior and support system engagement when grouped according to their profile
variable?
6.​ Is there a significant relationship between help-seeking behavior and the utilization of
available support systems among the respondents

METHODOLOGY

METHODOLOGY
Research Design
The research will utilize a quantitative research design that is descriptive-correlational.
The quantitative research design will be used to collect and analyze numerical data on students’
help-seeking behavior and their engagement with support systems. This approach will allow
objective measurement and comparison of responses, help identify patterns and trends and
examine the relationship between variables providing evidence-based insights into how different
support systems relate to students’ help-seeking practices and overall engagement.
The descriptive part of the study will present an overview of the prevalence and patterns
of help-seeking behavior among students, including the frequency of seeking assistance from
peers, faculty, and institutional support services such as Human Development Center,
Department of Student Services, Prefect of Discipline and Office of the Student Services and
Affairs. It will also assess the extent of engagement with these support systems, highlighting
which resources are most frequently used and identifying potential gaps in support utilization.
Descriptive statistics, such as frequency counts, percentages, means, and standard deviations,
will be employed to summarize these findings and present a clear profile of students’ behaviors
and support system engagement.

​

The correlational component will examine the strength and direction of the relationship
between students’ help-seeking behavior and their utilization of support systems. This analysis
will determine whether students who frequently seek help also engage more with available
support systems, and whether certain types of support are more strongly associated with
proactive help-seeking. Pearson’s correlation coefficient will be used for continuous and
normally distributed variables, while Spearman’s rho correlation will be applied for ordinal or

non-normally distributed variables. This approach will provide a comprehensive analysis into
how different support systems interact with students’ help-seeking practices, informing
strategies to improve the accessibility, effectiveness, and utilization of support services in the
University of La Salette, Inc.
Research Respondents
The respondents of this study will consist of freshman, sophomore and junior students
enrolled at the University of La Salette, Inc., Santiago City during the 2nd semester of the
academic year 2025-2026. The researchers will use a stratified random sampling technique to
select participants. The total student population will first be grouped according to their
respective academic programs or colleges, and respondents will then be randomly selected
from each group to ensure representation from different fields of study within the university.
Freshmen through juniors generally experience similar academic lives, such as
structured class schedules, coursework requirements and periodic examinations, which provide
relatively consistent learning environment. Seniors will be excluded from the study, as their
academic experience differs from that of the lower year levels. Seniors are typically involved in
internships, practicum, or off-campus training, which follow different schedules and learning
environments compared to regular classroom-based activities. By excluding seniors, the study
ensures that the participants have similar academic structures and schedules, providing more
consistent data on their help-seeking behavior and utilization of support systems. .

Locale of the study
This study will be conducted at the University of La Salette, Inc., Santiago City, during
the academic year 2025-2026. The participants of the study will be students enrolled in the
different colleges and academic departments of the university .

Research Instrument
​

This study will employ a quantitative method approach, utilizing quantitative data which

will be collected through survey questionnaires employing a five-point Likert scale ranging from
0-Never, 1-Rarely, 2- Sometimes, 3-Often, 4-Always . This scale will be used for data analysis.
The questionnaire will be composed of two main parts: Part I (Personal Details) and Part II
(Questionnaire Proper). Part I gathers basic information such as age, sex and year level. Part II
consists of six section designed to assess different aspects of students’ help-seeking behavior
and utilization of support services.
The Help-seeking Behavior section focuses on students willingness, frequency and comfort
in seeking assistance when faced with academic, emotional,or personal challenges.The Faculty
Support section evaluates the accessibility, responsiveness and effectiveness of faculty
members in providing academic guidance, clarification and emotional support to students. The
Human Development Center section examines students’ awareness, utilization and perception
of counseling and mental health services provided by the institution, including confidentiality,
accessibility and its impact on student well-being. The Department of Student Services section
assesses the extent to which students utilize institutional services for academic and personal
concerns, as well as their level of trust, comfort and reliance on these services. The Prefect of
Discipline section explores students’ engagement with disciplinary support systems, particularly
in seeking guidance related to rules, behavior, and student conduct and how these interactions
influence their help-seeking behavior. Lastly, the Office of Student Services and Affairs section
measures students’ awareness, participation and reliance on student-centered programs and
services that promote engagement, personal development, and overall support.

Data Collection Procedure
The researchers of the study will prepare a formal letter requesting permission to
conduct the study at the University of La Salette, Inc.. The letter will be addressed to the
University President and the respective College Deans to obtain approval for the conduct of the
study and the participation of students from different academic programs.
In gathering the data for the study, the researchers will create a printed survey
questionnaire to collect data from Freshman, Sophomore, and Junior year students of the
different colleges and program within the university. The questionnaire will consist of two parts:
demographic profile and the questionnaire proper. The questionnaire proper will include items
measuring students’ help-seeking behavior and their utilization of institutional support systems.
These are organized into following sections: Help-Seeking Behavior, Faculty Support, Human
Development Center (HDC), Department of Student Services (DSS), Prefect of Discipline (POD)
and Office of Student Services and Affairs (OSSA).
Before the full-scale data collection, a pilot test will be conducted with a small group of
representative students. This practice run will help identify and correct any unclear, confusing,
or biased items in the questionnaire, ensuring that the instrument is clear, reliable, and valid for
the target population. Feedback from the pilot test will be used to revise the questionnaire as
needed.
Using a stratified random sampling technique, participants will be randomly selected
from each year level to ensure proportional representation. Selected students will be invited to
participate, and participation will be voluntary. The researchers will provide clear instructions,
emphasizing that respondents can decline or withdraw at any time without any consequences.

The finalized questionnaires will be personally distributed to the selected participants in
classrooms or designated areas. Participants will be given approximately 3–5 minutes to
complete the survey. Researchers will personally retrieve the completed questionnaires to
ensure accuracy, completeness, and proper data handling.
To maintain confidentiality, all responses will be anonymized prior to analysis, and any
personal identifiers will be stored separately from the survey data. Completed questionnaires
will be securely kept in locked storage or encrypted digital files, accessible only to the
researchers.
This comprehensive data collection procedure ensures the gathering of reliable and
measurable data on the help-seeking behavior of students and the extent of their utilization with
support systems, enabling the researchers to examine the interrelationship between these
variables and provide evidence-based insights for improving student support services.
Data Analysis Procedure
The collected data will be organized, coded, and analyzed using both descriptive and
inferential statistical methods. Descriptive statistics, such as frequency counts, percentages,
means, and standard deviations, will be used to describe the respondents’ demographic profile,
help-seeking behavior and the extent of utilization of support systems such as family, peers,
faculty, human development and online platforms.
To examine the interrelationships between the help-seeking and support systems among
the university students, Pearson’s correlation coefficient will be employed for variables that are
continuous and normally distributed based on the combined mean scores obtained from the
Likert-scale items for each domain. For variables that are ordinal or not normally distributed,
Spearman’s rho will be applied to assess the strength and direction of monotonic relationships.

This approach will enable the researchers to assess help-seeking behavior and
utilization with support systems among students, providing evidence-based insights to enhance
student support services.
